Step 1: Is this tool right for your project?

This worksheet uses the Florida Building Code simplified method (FBC Residential Code Table R301.2(2)) for determining wind pressures on window and door openings. It works only for a specific kind of project.

Your project must meet ALL of these criteria:

  • Single-family detached home. Not a condo, townhouse, duplex, mobile home, or commercial building.
  • Single story. Mean roof height 30 feet or less (measured from grade to average roof height).
  • Simple shape. Rectangular floor plan with a normal gable or hip roof. Not L-shaped, not multi-level, no significant additions or wings.
  • Enclosed building. Has walls on all sides with normal-sized openings. Not an open carport, pavilion, or pole barn.
  • Not in HVHZ. Property is NOT in Miami-Dade County, Broward County, or Monroe County (the High-Velocity Hurricane Zone). These counties have stricter requirements and require a Miami-Dade NOA plus PE-sealed installation engineering.
  • Not directly on the coast. Property is NOT within 600 feet of the Atlantic Ocean or Gulf of Mexico shoreline (Exposure D conditions).
  • You own and live in the home. It's your primary residence and the property is in your personal name (not an LLC, trust, or rental). This is required for the Florida owner-builder exemption (FS 489.103(7)).
  • Replacing 25% or less of your windows/doors per year. If you replace more than 25% of the glazed opening area in a 12-month period and your house was permitted before March 1, 2002, you trigger additional opening-protection requirements.

Why these restrictions?

The simplified method (FBC R301.2(2)) is a pre-calculated lookup table the Florida Building Code provides for typical residential cases. Outside its scope — multi-story buildings, complex geometries, HVHZ counties, coastal Exposure D, open buildings — the table doesn't apply and a site-specific engineering analysis is legally required.

This tool intentionally refuses to operate outside the simplified-method scope to keep you on the right side of the code and the law (Florida Statute Ch. 471 governing the practice of engineering).

How do I tell Exposure B from Exposure C?

Exposure B: Suburban or urban areas, wooded areas, neighborhoods with single-family houses and trees in all four directions for at least 1,500 feet from your property. Most inland Florida neighborhoods.

Exposure C: Flat open terrain with scattered obstructions less than 30 ft tall. Includes flat open country, grasslands, and many areas near (but not directly on) the water. When in doubt, use C — it's more conservative.

Exposure D (within 600 ft of open ocean) is NOT supported by this tool. If your home is directly coastal, you need a PE.

Step 3: List your windows and doors

For each opening you plan to replace, enter its location, size, and the product you intend to install. The tool will compute the required wind pressure (from FBC R301.2(2)) and check it against your product's tested design pressure.

What you need before starting

  • Width and height of each opening (rough opening size, in inches)
  • The Florida Product Approval number (FL#) for the window/door you plan to install — look it up at floridabuilding.org
  • The tested design pressure (DP) from the product approval — looks like "+45/-50 psf"
What is "Effective Wind Area" (EWA)?

EWA is the surface area used to determine wind pressure for a window or door. For our purposes, EWA = width × height of the opening (in square feet). Larger openings have lower required pressures than smaller ones because wind pressure averages out over bigger surfaces.

What's the difference between Zone 4 and Zone 5?

Zone 4 = interior wall. Windows and doors in the middle portion of any wall.

Zone 5 = corner. Windows and doors within the "corner width" of a building corner. Wind pressures (especially negative/suction) are higher here because wind accelerates around corners.

Step 4: Your Worksheet

Review the worksheet below. If everything looks correct, print it (or save as PDF) and submit it with your permit application along with the supporting documents listed at the bottom.

Before you submit
  • Verify with your local building department that they accept the FBC R301.2(2) simplified method for residential window replacement. Most non-HVHZ Florida jurisdictions do.
  • Attach your Florida Product Approval document(s) showing the manufacturer's tested DPs.
  • Complete the Owner/Builder Affidavit required by your jurisdiction (downloadable from your building department's website).
  • If your project total exceeds $2,500, file a Notice of Commencement with your county clerk before the first inspection.
  • This worksheet does not replace local department requirements. If your jurisdiction has its own worksheet format, you may need to transfer these values to their form.
